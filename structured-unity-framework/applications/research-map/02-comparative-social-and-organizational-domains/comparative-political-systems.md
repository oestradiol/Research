# Comparative Political Systems

## Role in the applications atlas

This node provides the bounded comparative-political backdrop needed when case differences may arise from institutional design rather than only from crisis management choices.

## Why SUF applies here

SUF can compare differently structured political systems if the route stays honest about unit mismatch, authority distribution, and scale differences.

## Main objects of study

- executive-legislative configurations
- centralization versus federalism
- party systems and coalition dynamics
- administrative traditions and intergovernmental structures

## Typical units and unitization problems

Units may include constitutional layers, executive centres, ministries, or jurisdictions. The persistent problem is commensurability: different systems often do not offer one-to-one unit matches.

## Typical scales

- micro: institutional veto points or decision channels
- meso: regime-level coordination patterns
- macro: comparative system architectures across countries

## Likely I / C / L / U relevance

This node most strongly shapes interpretation. It helps explain why the same apparent `I`, `C`, or `L` signature may mean different things in unitary and federal settings.

## One concrete SUF research question

What minimum political-system adjustment is needed before a New Zealand route can be compared responsibly with a Taiwanese or Australian route?

## One bounded future route

A short comparator note on unitary versus federal coordination topology in New Zealand and Australia.
